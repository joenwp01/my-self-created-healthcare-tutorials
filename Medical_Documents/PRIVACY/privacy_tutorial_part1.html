De-Identification — General Concepts

De-identification is the process of removing or transforming information in a dataset so individuals cannot be readily identified. It enables secondary use of healthcare data — research, analytics, public health — without exposing patient privacy.

De-identification is not anonymization. Anonymization implies identity can never be recovered. De-identification reduces re-identification risk to an acceptable level. This distinction matters legally and ethically under HIPAA.

Three Categories of Data Fields

Identifier Classification

Direct Identifiers — Alone or nearly alone they identify an individual: names, SSNs, MRNs, phone, email, full addresses, biometrics. These are suppressed (removed entirely).

Quasi-Identifiers (QI) — Individually innocuous but identifying in combination: DOB, ZIP, sex, age, race/ethnicity. These are generalized (made less precise) to preserve analytical utility while reducing linkability.

Sensitive Attributes — The information we want to analyze: diagnoses, labs, medications. Retained but only after QIs are sufficiently anonymized.

Three De-Identification Techniques

TechniqueWhat It Does & When to Apply
SuppressionCompletely removes a field. Applied to direct identifiers and records that cannot meet privacy standards through generalization alone.
GeneralizationReplaces precise values with broader categories. DOB → year; 5-digit ZIP → 3-digit prefix; exact age → decade. Preserves utility while reducing linkability.
PerturbationAdds controlled random noise to numeric values. Used in differential privacy (Part 2, Station 11). Useful for aggregate statistics where exact counts must be obscured.

HIPAA Safe Harbor — The 18 Identifier Rule

HIPAA provides two de-identification pathways. Safe Harbor lists exactly 18 categories of identifiers that must be removed. No statistical expertise required — remove all 18 and the dataset is compliant. The second pathway, Expert Determination, allows a statistician to certify "very small" risk without removing every identifier, but requires formal analysis (Station 03).

Three Critical Safe Harbor Rules

Rule 1 — ZIP Code Population Threshold

3-digit ZIP prefixes may be retained only if the area contains ≥ 20,000 people (U.S. Census). If fewer than 20,000, the prefix must become 000. Very rural ZIPs have so few residents that even a 3-digit prefix can uniquely identify someone.

Rule 2 — Date Generalization & Age 90+ Rule

All patient-associated dates (admission, discharge, birth, death, procedure) must be reduced to year only. Patients aged 90 or older must also have their birth year removed, replaced with Age 90+. A rare birth year can uniquely identify a very long-lived individual.

Rule 3 — The Limited Data Set (LDS) Alternative

A Limited Data Set removes only 16 of the most identifying fields but retains dates and geographic units smaller than state. An LDS requires a signed Data Use Agreement (DUA) and is only for research, public health, or care operations. It is NOT Safe Harbor — less protection, greater utility.

Re-Identification Risk — Prosecutor, Journalist & Marketer Models

De-identification removes identifiers but does not prove the dataset is safe. Re-identification risk quantification proves it — by modeling what different adversaries could accomplish with released data. Three attacker models are standard in the field, and a dataset can appear low-risk under one model while failing another.

Sweeney's 87% Finding

Three Fields Can Identify 87% of Americans

In 1997, Latanya Sweeney (Harvard) demonstrated that 87% of the U.S. population could be uniquely identified using only 5-digit ZIP + full date of birth + sex, cross-referenced against public voter registration rolls. Important caveats: this is population uniqueness, used 1990 census data, and used 5-digit ZIP — HIPAA's 3-digit prefix significantly reduces this risk. The lesson: quasi-identifiers combine explosively.

Sample vs. Population Uniqueness

A record unique within a released sample may not be unique in the full population. If sampling fraction = f, approximate population uniqueness ≈ sample uniqueness × f. Small samples of large populations are harder to re-identify because an attacker cannot know whether a sample-unique record corresponds to a population-unique individual.

The Three Attacker Models

Prosecutor Model

Attacker already knows a specific individual is in the dataset and attempts to find their record. Worst case — targeted attack, litigation, or investigation.

Risk = 1 / min(k)

Even one k=1 record makes prosecutor risk = 1.0 (100%).

Journalist Model

Attacker samples randomly from the dataset and tries to re-identify whoever they land on. Models investigative reporting or spot-check re-identification.

Risk = (#k=1 records) / N

Fraction of the dataset that is perfectly re-identifiable.

Marketer Model

Attacker maximizes total re-identifications across the dataset. Models commercial data brokers or bulk linkage attacks.

Risk = (1/N) × Σ(1/kᵢ)

Average success rate across all N records.

HIPAA Expert Determination Threshold

"Very small" risk is interpreted in practice as ≤ 0.09 (9%) under the prosecutor model. This means min(k) ≥ 12 (since 1/12 ≈ 0.083 < 0.09). Many organizations target k ≥ 5 for research datasets.

Before any transformation, every patient is k=1. All three risk metrics equal 1.0 — maximum possible risk.

k-Anonymity — Equivalence Classes

k-Anonymity ensures every record is indistinguishable from at least k−1 other records on all quasi-identifier fields. A group of k records sharing the same QI values is an equivalence class. An attacker who links someone to a class faces 1-in-k uncertainty.

k-Anonymity Formal DefinitionDataset D satisfies k-anonymity iff for every record rᵢ, equivalence class Q(rᵢ) contains ≥ k records.
Q(rᵢ) = { rⱼ ∈ D : QI(rᵢ) = QI(rⱼ) }
Two Tools to Achieve k-Anonymity

Generalization — Replace precise QI values with broader categories to merge records into the same class. DOB→decade, ZIP→3-digit. Cost: reduced data precision.

Suppression — Remove records that cannot be merged into a group of size k by generalization. Last resort; costs an entire data point.

l-Diversity — Protecting Sensitive Attributes Within Groups

k-Anonymity ensures an attacker cannot single out a specific individual — but says nothing about what they can infer about that individual's sensitive attributes. l-Diversity plugs this gap: within each equivalence class, there must be at least l distinct values of every sensitive attribute.

l-Diversity DefinitionAn equivalence class Q satisfies l-diversity if:
|{ distinct sensitive values in Q }| ≥ l
A dataset is l-diverse if every equivalence class satisfies this.
The Homogeneity Attack — Why k-Anonymity Alone Fails

If all patients in an equivalence class share the same diagnosis — say, all five have Bipolar Disorder — an attacker linking a person to that group immediately knows their diagnosis, even without singling them out. k-Anonymity provided k=5 "protection" but zero attribute privacy. l-Diversity prevents this by demanding sensitive values be spread across the group.

COUNTER-EXAMPLE — Homogeneity Failure
If Group β were {Yolanda=T2DM, Priya=T2DM}: l=1. Attacker knows with 100% certainty the patient has T2DM — even with k=2. Fix: generalize further to merge groups, or replace one record with a patient carrying a different diagnosis.
l=1 is a complete failure of attribute privacy despite k-anonymity. This is extremely common with prevalent conditions (hypertension, T2DM) that dominate certain demographic groups.
