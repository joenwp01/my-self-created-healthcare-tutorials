De-Identification — Field Classification & Transformation Burden

De-identification is the process of removing or transforming information so individuals cannot be readily identified. At the intermediate level, we move past identifying single fields and instead quantify the transformation burden across a full dataset — counting how many cells are suppressed, generalized, or retained, and computing the resulting information loss as a percentage.

Three Categories — Quick Reference

Direct Identifiers — alone identify an individual: names, SSNs, MRNs, phone, email, full addresses, biometrics. Action: suppress.

Quasi-Identifiers (QI) — identifying in combination: date of birth, ZIP code, sex, age, race. Action: generalize.

Sensitive Attributes — clinical data we want to analyze: diagnoses, labs, medications. Action: retain.

Information Loss MetricTotal cells = N×F (N records, F fields per record)
Information-loss rate = (suppressed_cells + generalized_cells) / total_cells
A higher loss rate reduces re-identification risk but also reduces analytical utility.

HIPAA Safe Harbor — Edge Cases & Aggregate Compliance Burden

HIPAA Safe Harbor requires removal of 18 categories of identifiers, with three numerical rules that introduce real arithmetic on real records: ZIP populations <20,000 must be replaced with 000, exact birth dates must be reduced to year only, and patients aged ≥90 must have their birth year replaced with “Age 90+”. At the intermediate level, we apply these rules across a cohort and compute the aggregate triggering rate.

Three Numerical Safe Harbor RulesRule 1: 3-digit ZIP prefix retained only if total population in that prefix ≥ 20,000
Rule 2: All birth dates → year only (month and day suppressed)
Rule 3: If patient age ≥ 90, replace birth year with “Age 90+” bucket
Why the 20,000-Person Threshold

The Census-derived rule prevents very small geographies from re-introducing identifiability. A 3-digit ZIP prefix with only ~5,000 residents combined with sex and a specific birth year may yield only a handful of matches in a voter file. The 20,000 minimum keeps the anonymity set large enough that the residual QI combination {sex, birth year, ZIP-3} alone cannot single out an individual under typical Census distributions.

📌 Scenario

A community health network has a 500-patient cohort serving urban Seattle (ZIP-3 prefix 981, population ~700,000) and rural Walla Walla County (ZIP-3 prefix 993, population ~18,500). Approximately 4% of patients are aged 90 or older. Compute the total number of records affected by each Safe Harbor numerical rule.

⚙ How to Apply Each Rule
  • Rule 1 (ZIP population): any record from a ZIP-3 region with pop < 20,000 needs that prefix replaced with 000. ZIP 981 → KEEP. ZIP 993 → REPLACE.
  • Rule 2 (year-only DOB): applies to ALL records, always. So 500/500 records are affected.
  • Rule 3 (Age 90+): 4% × 500 = 20 records.
Suppose 380 patients live in ZIP-3 prefix 981 and 120 in ZIP-3 prefix 993.

Rule 1: ZIP 993 has pop 18,500 < 20,000 → 120 records need ZIP prefix replaced with 000.
Rule 2: ALL records have full DOB → 500 records have DOB reduced to year only.
Rule 3: 4% × 500 = 20 records with Age 90+ replacement.

Records triggering BOTH Rule 1 and Rule 3 (overlap): 4% × 120 ≈ 5 records.
Records triggering at least one of the three rules: 500 (because Rule 2 applies to all).
🔎 Practical Note

Even though Rule 2 applies universally, organizations usually batch their Safe Harbor pipeline in three passes (one per rule) to make audit logs cleaner. Rule 1 lookups require a current Census-derived ZIP-3 population table; many compliance failures stem from using stale lookup tables when a ZIP's population dropped below 20,000 since the last refresh.

💡 Aggregate Safe Harbor compliance is binary: either all 18 categories are addressed for every record, or the dataset is non-compliant. The intermediate-level question is not “compliant?” but “how many records were affected by which rule?” — useful for audit trails and information-loss accounting.

Re-Identification Risk — Three Attacker Models + Bayesian Attacker

De-identification removes identifiers; re-identification risk quantification proves the dataset is safe by computing how likely an adversary could link a released record back to a specific individual. The intermediate exercise computes the three standardized attacker metrics (Prosecutor, Journalist, Marketer) across a multi-group cohort. The advanced-intermediate exercise treats re-identification as a Bayesian inference problem — the attacker starts with a prior 1/N and updates via likelihood ratios for each matching QI field.

The Three Attacker Models — Quick Reference

Prosecutor Model
Risk = 1 / min(k)

Attacker targets a known individual. Even one k=1 record makes risk = 1.0.

Journalist Model
Risk = (#k=1 records) / N

Random selection; fraction of dataset trivially exposable.

Marketer Model
Risk = (1/N) × Σ(1/kᵢ)

Average linkage success when attacker tries every record.

HIPAA Expert Determination Threshold

“Very small” risk under HIPAA is interpreted in practice as ≤ 0.09 (9%). Prosecutor risk requires min(k) ≥ 12 (since 1/12 ≈ 0.083). All three metrics must pass before release.

🔎 Marketer Has a Neat Property

The marketer risk simplifies to (number of equivalence groups) / N when each group contributes exactly 1 to the sum, which it always does since k_group × (1/k_group) = 1. So Marketer = G/N where G is the count of distinct QI combinations. For this dataset: 4/24 ≈ 0.167.

📌 The Bayesian View of Re-Identification

The three attacker models in Focused Tutorial A treat re-identification as a structural property of the dataset (group sizes). A complementary — and more powerful — view treats it as a Bayesian inference problem: the attacker starts with uncertainty (a prior of 1/N for any specific record being a known target) and updates that belief each time a known QI value of the target matches a record's value. This perspective unifies re-identification with probabilistic record linkage (Station 09) and clinical Bayesian reasoning (Station 08).

⚙ The Model
  • Hypothesis H: “This released record belongs to the attacker's known target.”
  • Prior P(H): 1/N where N is the size of the released dataset. Prior odds ≈ 1/(N−1) ≈ 1/N for large N.
  • Evidence Eᵢ: “Field i of this record matches the target's known value for field i.”
  • Likelihood ratio LRᵢ = mᵢ/uᵢ where mᵢ = P(field i matches | record IS the target) (very close to 1 for stable fields), uᵢ = P(field i matches | record is NOT the target) = the marginal frequency of that value in the population.
  • Posterior odds = prior odds × ∏ LRᵢ (independence assumption on the fields).
  • P(re-id) = posterior odds / (1 + posterior odds). Compare to 0.09 threshold.
Bayesian Re-Identification — Odds FormPrior odds ≈ 1 / (N − 1)
For each matching QI field: LRᵢ = mᵢ / uᵢ
Posterior odds = prior odds × ∏ LRᵢ
P(re-id) = posterior odds / (1 + posterior odds)
WORKED EXAMPLE — Re-ID Attack on a 1,200-Patient Cohort
Setup: Released dataset N=1,200. Target known to be in dataset. Attacker has 3 QI values for target: Sex=F, Age band 60–69, ZIP-3=981.

Step 1: Prior odds = 1/1,199 ≈ 0.000834
Step 2: LR for each matching field:
  LR_sex = m/u = 0.99/0.52 ≈ 1.904 (sex is binary — weak evidence)
  LR_age = 0.95/0.12 ≈ 7.917 (10 age bands → moderate evidence)
  LR_zip3 = 0.92/0.08 ≈ 11.5 (geographic specificity — strong evidence)
Step 3: Combined LR = 1.904 × 7.917 × 11.5 ≈ 173.4
Step 4: Posterior odds = 0.000834 × 173.4 ≈ 0.1446
Step 5: P(re-id) = 0.1446 / 1.1446 ≈ 0.1263 (≈ 12.6%) → FAILS 9% threshold
🔎 Why This View Matters

The three classical attacker models give a single number per dataset; the Bayesian model gives a number per attacker scenario (target identity + observable QI vector). This is more granular and matches how real re-identification studies are reported in the academic literature. Notice that the LR values multiplicatively compound — this is the same mechanism that drives Sweeney's 87% result (ZIP+DOB+Sex uniquely identifies 87% of U.S. residents). Each independent field acts as a separate Bayesian update, and the prior is rapidly overwhelmed by the cumulative evidence.

💡 The Bayesian model is also the mathematical foundation for differential privacy's membership inference defense: DP bounds the maximum LR per query at eε, so no matter how many queries an attacker observes, the posterior cannot deviate from the prior by more than a known factor. This is why DP is the only method providing protection regardless of auxiliary knowledge.

k-Anonymity — Equivalence Class Sizing & Suppression Strategy

k-Anonymity requires every record to be indistinguishable from at least k−1 others on all quasi-identifier fields. At the intermediate level we solve the strategic problem: given a target k, which groups must be suppressed (or merged), what is the suppression rate, and what is the resulting min(k)?

k-Anonymity — Decision Procedure for Target k*For each equivalence group G with size k_G:
  If k_G ≥ k* → keep all records in G
  If k_G < k* → suppress all records in G (or merge with adjacent group)
Suppression rate = (suppressed records) / N
New min(k) = smallest k_G among retained groups
The Suppression Trade-Off

Higher target k = stronger identity protection but higher suppression. Production releases typically target k=5 for most research datasets and k=12 for highly sensitive (HIV, SUD, mental health) datasets. Reducing QI granularity (e.g., 10-year age bands instead of 5-year) before applying k-anonymity is almost always preferable to suppression — it preserves more records.

l-Diversity — Counting Distinct Values & Detecting Skewness

k-Anonymity protects identity; l-Diversity protects sensitive attributes. Each equivalence class must contain at least l distinct values of the sensitive attribute. At intermediate level we go beyond “does it meet l=2?” and compute the maximum attacker certainty per class — the highest fraction held by any single value — which exposes the skewness attack that simple distinct-value counting misses.

l-Diversity & Maximum Attacker Certaintyl(Q) = | { distinct sensitive values in Q } |
P_max(Q) = max(count of any single value) / |Q|
Class passes if l(Q) ≥ l* AND P_max(Q) is acceptable (often P_max < 0.5 in practice)
The Skewness Attack — What l Alone Misses

A class of size 5 with diagnoses {Diabetes, Diabetes, Diabetes, Diabetes, Bipolar} satisfies l=2 (two distinct values) — but the attacker can infer “80% likely Diabetes” with near-certainty. l-Diversity counts distinct values; t-closeness goes further by requiring distributions to be balanced. Computing P_max gives a practical proxy.

📌 Scenario

A hospital privacy officer reviews two equivalence classes from a k-anonymized release. Class Alpha has 4 patients with diagnoses {HTN, HTN, HTN, T2DM}. Class Beta has 5 patients with diagnoses {Bipolar, OUD, T2DM, HIV, Cancer}. She computes l and P_max for each.

  Distinct values = {HTN, T2DM} → l = 2 → satisfies l=2 ✓
  Counts: HTN=3, T2DM=1 → P_max = 3/4 = 0.75 (75%)
  Attacker certainty: 75% → skewness flag ❌

  Distinct values = 5 → l = 5
  Counts: 1 each → P_max = 1/5 = 0.20 (20%)
  Attacker certainty: 20% → well-balanced ✓
🔎 Diagnostic Outcome

Class Alpha passes the formal l=2 criterion but exposes the dominant attribute (HTN) at 75% certainty. Class Beta passes both criteria. Remediations for Alpha: (a) merge with an adjacent class to balance the distribution, (b) suppress one HTN record, dropping counts to {2, 1} with P_max = 2/3 ≈ 67%, or (c) require l ≥ 3 in the DUA so the small-cell skew problem is structurally prevented.

💡 In real datasets the dominant value problem is most acute for common diagnoses (HTN, T2DM) that saturate small classes by base rate. Disease-specific bucketing (e.g., grouping all cardiovascular dx into one bucket) at the dataset level can reduce dominance without losing too much utility.

Conditional Probability — Stepwise Anonymity Set Narrowing

Conditional probability models how an attacker progressively narrows the anonymity set as each QI field is matched. Starting from a large population, each field acts as a multiplicative filter on remaining candidates: N→N×p₁→N×p₁×p₂→... When the residual count approaches 1, the record is effectively re-identified.

Stepwise Anonymity ReductionN_remaining = N × P(field₁ matches) × P(field₂ matches) × …
P(re-id) ≈ 1 / N_remaining
If fields are independent, each one acts as an additional multiplicative reducer.
Why Each Field Compresses Geometrically

If sex is 50/50, age band has 10 values, county has 8 values, and dx category has 12 values, applying all four to a 25,000-record dataset takes us through 25,000 → 12,500 → 1,250 → 156 → 13. By the fourth field, the original dataset is compressed by a factor of nearly 2,000. This is why Sweeney's finding (87% U.S. uniquely identified by 3 fields) is so robust: the compression is exponential in the number of fields.

Unicity — Multi-Field Cell Occupancy Analysis

Unicity measures average records per quasi-identifier combination cell across the dataset. At intermediate level we extend it: how does the addition of a 4th or 5th QI field collapse unicity? Because each new field multiplies the total cell count, average occupancy falls geometrically — and when occupancy drops below 1, most records become unique fingerprints.

Unicity & k-Anonymity InferenceTotal cells = ∏ᵢ Sᵢ (product of distinct values per field)
Avg occupancy u = N / Total cells
If u < 1 → most occupied cells are singletons (high unicity, high re-id risk)
If u ≥ 5 → most cells have ≥ 2 records (lower risk, but rare combinations may still be unique)
From u ≥ 5 to u < 1 — Two Fields Can Tip the Balance

Going from 3 QIs to 4 QIs (e.g., adding diagnosis chapter with 15 values) multiplies cells by 15. Going from 4 to 5 (e.g., adding admission month with 12 values) multiplies by another 12. In just two added fields, total cells grow by 180× — if N stays fixed, occupancy collapses by the same factor. This is why generalization (reducing Sᵢ) is the most important countermeasure.

💡 Each QI field is a multiplier on total cells. The way to think about unicity strategy is multiplicatively: which field, when its S is divided in half (or by 4), gives the best occupancy improvement? Pick the field where you can lose granularity with the least loss of analytical value.

Bayes' Theorem — Sequential Updates via Odds Form

The classical Bayes formula P(H|E) = P(E|H)×P(H) / P(E) requires recomputing the full denominator after each new piece of evidence. The odds form — posterior odds = prior odds × likelihood ratio — makes sequential evidence chains effortless: multiply each LR in turn, then convert back to probability at the end. This is the workhorse of two-stage clinical screening and the mathematical heart of attacker re-identification.

Bayes' Theorem — Odds FormPrior odds = P(H) / P(¬H)
For each independent evidence Eᵢ: LRᵢ = P(Eᵢ|H) / P(Eᵢ|¬H)
Posterior odds (after k pieces of evidence) = Prior odds × LR₁ × LR₂ × … × LRₖ
Convert: P(H | all evidence) = posterior odds / (1 + posterior odds)
Why Odds Form is Powerful

Each LR captures the diagnostic value of one test as a single number: LR>1 supports H, LR<1 contradicts H, LR=1 is uninformative. Two independent tests with LR₁=8 and LR₂=20 combine to LR_total = 160. The prior is updated once at the end. This is much cleaner than running the full Bayes equation twice. It also matches how clinicians actually reason: “a positive antibody (LR=10) and a positive PCR (LR=30) together give 300× the evidence of a single test”.

📌 Scenario

A hospital screens for active TB. Local prevalence = 3%. A patient receives two independent tests: Test 1 (skin) has sensitivity 0.85, FPR 0.10; Test 2 (blood assay) has sensitivity 0.92, FPR 0.03. Both come back positive. Compute the posterior probability of TB using the odds-form method.

⚙ Method
  • Prior odds = prevalence / (1 − prevalence)
  • LR per test = sensitivity / FPR
  • Multiply prior odds by every LR (one per test)
  • Convert: P = odds / (1 + odds)
OPERATION — Apply the Odds Form
Step 1 — Prior odds = 0.03 / 0.97 ≈ 0.03093
Step 2 — LRs:
  LR₁ = 0.85 / 0.10 = 8.5
  LR₂ = 0.92 / 0.03 ≈ 30.67
Step 3 — Posterior odds = 0.03093 × 8.5 × 30.67 ≈ 8.063
Step 4 — P(TB | both +) = 8.063 / 9.063 ≈ 0.890 (≈89.0%)
🔎 Why the Posterior Is So High

Two near-independent tests with high LRs combine multiplicatively, overwhelming a low base rate. Combined LR = 261 — even with only a 3% prior, the posterior reaches 89%. Without the second test, a single positive skin test would give posterior ≈ 21% (just enough for clinical concern but not certainty). This is why two-stage screening protocols are standard: the multiplicative combination of LRs is more powerful than any single test.

💡 Parallel to re-identification: an attacker observes one matching field (LR ≈ 10), then another (LR ≈ 20), then a third (LR ≈ 100) — combined LR = 20,000 × prior odds = posterior odds. Each independent matching field is a separate Bayesian update. Re-id risk grows multiplicatively with QI count.

Fellegi–Sunter Linkage — Mixed Agreement & Bayesian Posterior

Fellegi–Sunter (F–S) is the clinical-informatics standard for probabilistic record linkage when datasets lack a shared identifier. The intermediate exercise handles mixed agreement (some fields agree, some disagree) by giving disagreeing fields a negative weight. The advanced-intermediate exercise reframes the whole problem in Bayesian terms: prior odds derived from population overlap, multiplied by per-field likelihood ratios, give a true match probability — revealing that even a large F–S score may translate to only a moderate posterior when the prior is small.

🔎 Why Disagreement Penalties Are Asymmetric

A disagreeing field's weight depends on how unusual the disagreement is for a true match. The asymmetry encodes domain knowledge about which fields are stable and which fluctuate.

📌 Why a Bayesian View Adds Value

The classical F–S score W is a log-odds measure but doesn't tell you the actual probability that a candidate pair is a match. Two candidate pairs with the same W can have very different posterior match probabilities if their priors differ. The Bayesian formulation makes this explicit by combining (a) a population-level prior (how many true matches exist among all possible pairs) with (b) per-field LRs (the same evidence used in F–S). The result — a posterior probability — tells the analyst whether the link is genuinely confident, ambiguous, or weak.

⚙ The Model
  • Total possible pairs = |A| × |B|, where A and B are the two datasets being linked.
  • Estimated true matches = M_true (often estimated from a pilot linkage or domain knowledge of overlap, e.g., %patients seen at both sites).
  • Prior probability of match P(H) = M_true / (|A| × |B|). For large datasets this is very small.
  • Prior odds P(H)/P(¬H) ≈ P(H) when P(H) is small.
  • Likelihood ratio per agreeing field LRᵢ = mᵢ / uᵢ.
  • Posterior odds = prior odds × ∏ LRᵢ.
  • P(match | data) = posterior odds / (1 + posterior odds).

P(match) = posterior odds / (1 + posterior odds)

In Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage (PPRL) via Bloom filters, each name is hashed to a bit array that can be compared without revealing the underlying value. At intermediate level we move beyond a single Dice score and compare multiple candidates against one source, using both Dice and Jaccard similarity to triangulate the best match.

Dice and Jaccard for Bloom Filter Bit ArraysDice = 2 × |A ∩ B| / (|A| + |B|)
Jaccard = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|, where |A ∪ B| = |A| + |B| − |A ∩ B|
Relationship: Jaccard = Dice / (2 − Dice)
Decision: Dice ≥ 0.90 → match; 0.70 ≤ Dice < 0.90 → review; Dice < 0.70 → non-match
Why Compute Both Dice and Jaccard?

Dice and Jaccard rank candidates the same way but spread their values differently. Dice is more forgiving of small differences (it's the harmonic mean’s cousin), while Jaccard penalizes union mismatches more sharply. When |A| and |B| differ substantially, Jaccard is often more diagnostic. Most production systems use one or the other; computing both provides a sanity check.

Differential Privacy — Single Queries & Sequential Composition

Differential Privacy (DP) adds calibrated Laplace noise to query results so no single individual's data meaningfully affects the output. The intermediate exercise computes the per-query noise scale across multiple isolated queries with different epsilons. The advanced-intermediate exercise applies the sequential composition theorem to allocate a fixed total budget across many queries — revealing the trade-off between query count and per-query utility.

Laplace Mechanism & CompositionM(D) = f(D) + Laplace(0, Δf/ε)
Noise scale b = Δf / ε (Δf = 1 for count queries)
Noise std dev = √2 × b
Sequential composition: ε_total = ε₁ + ε₂ + … + εₖ
Why Composition Matters

Each DP query consumes from the total privacy budget. Answering 10 queries at ε=1 each gives total ε=10 — weak privacy. The analyst must decide: many queries at lower ε per query (more noise, more questions answered) or fewer queries at higher ε (less noise, fewer questions). Small counts suffer disproportionately because relative noise = (noise std dev) / true count grows as true count shrinks.

🔎 Why Co-Diagnosis Counts Are Hard

Co-diagnosis counts (HCV+HIV, OUD+pregnancy, etc.) often have high social stigma and small true values — both push toward smaller ε. But the same parameters that protect privacy destroy utility. Real-world solutions: combine multiple rare categories into a single “other” bucket, or use a Gaussian mechanism with smarter composition (RDP) that requires less noise for the same privacy.

💡 The sensitivity Δf = 1 for any count query because adding/removing one person changes the count by exactly 1. For sum queries on unbounded values, Δf equals the value range, often requiring much larger b and dramatically more noise.

📌 The Composition Problem

Real-world analysts rarely ask one DP query — they ask many. The sequential composition theorem states that answering k queries with budgets ε₁, ε₂, …, εₖ on the same dataset consumes total budget ε_total = ε₁ + ε₂ + … + εₖ. Given a fixed total budget (often set by institutional policy at 1–3), the analyst must allocate it across queries. More queries = less ε per query = more noise. Small counts suffer disproportionately because relative noise grows as true count shrinks.

⚙ Allocation Strategies
  • Uniform: ε_i = ε_total / k for every query. Simple, fair, but equally noisy.
  • Non-uniform: larger ε for queries with small true counts (where noise hurts most), smaller ε for large counts.
  • Aggregation: combine multiple small categories into one larger category before querying.
  • Small-cell suppression: refuse to publish counts below a threshold (e.g., < 10) and reallocate the budget.

The Bayesian Through-Line

Three stations — Re-ID (Station 03), Linkage (Station 09), and DP (Station 11) — each have an advanced-intermediate exercise that uses the same Bayesian skeleton: prior × likelihood ratios → posterior. In re-identification, the prior is 1/N and the LRs come from QI-field match rates. In record linkage, the prior is the overlap fraction and the LRs come from F–S m/u ratios. In DP, the bound on posterior shift is eε per query, and composition forces the analyst to budget that shift across queries. Recognizing the Bayesian skeleton across all three is the central intermediate-level insight.
