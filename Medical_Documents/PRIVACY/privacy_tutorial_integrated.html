De-Identification — General Concepts

De-identification is the process of removing or transforming information in a dataset so individuals cannot be readily identified. It enables secondary use of healthcare data — research, analytics, public health — without exposing patient privacy.

De-identification is not the same as anonymization. Anonymization implies identity can never be recovered. De-identification only reduces re-identification risk to an acceptable level — a legally and ethically meaningful distinction under HIPAA.

Three Categories of Data Fields

Identifier Classification Framework

Direct Identifiers — Alone or nearly alone they identify an individual: names, SSNs, MRNs, phone, email, full addresses, biometrics. Action: suppress (remove entirely).

Quasi-Identifiers (QI) — Individually innocuous but identifying in combination: date of birth, ZIP code, sex, age, race. Action: generalize (make less precise) to preserve utility while reducing linkability.

Sensitive Attributes — The clinical data we want to analyze: diagnoses, labs, medications. Action: retain, but only after QIs are sufficiently anonymized.

Three De-Identification Techniques

TechniqueWhat It Does & When to Apply
SuppressionCompletely removes a field or entire record. Applied to direct identifiers and records that cannot meet privacy standards through generalization alone (k=1 outliers).
GeneralizationReplaces precise values with broader categories: DOB → year only; 5-digit ZIP → 3-digit prefix; exact age → age decade. Preserves analytical utility while reducing linkability.
PerturbationAdds controlled random noise to numeric values. Used in Differential Privacy (Station 11). Best for aggregate statistics where exact individual counts must be obscured.

HIPAA Safe Harbor — The 18 Identifier Rule

HIPAA provides two de-identification pathways. Safe Harbor is the mechanical approach: remove exactly 18 categories of identifiers and the dataset is legally de-identified — no statistician required. The second pathway, Expert Determination, allows a qualified statistician to certify the risk is "very small" using a formal analysis (Station 03), but requires documented methodology.

Three Critical Safe Harbor Rules

Rule 1 — ZIP Code Population Threshold

3-digit ZIP prefixes may be retained only if the geographic area they cover contains ≥ 20,000 people per U.S. Census data. If fewer than 20,000, the prefix must become 000. Very rural ZIP areas have so few residents that even a 3-digit prefix can narrow down an individual to a handful of candidates.

Rule 2 — Date Generalization & the Age 90+ Rule

All patient-associated dates (admission, discharge, birth, death, procedure) must be reduced to year only. Patients aged 90 or older must additionally have their birth year replaced with the label Age 90+. Why? A rare birth year (e.g., 1928) in a small population can uniquely identify a very long-lived individual even after other fields are anonymized.

Rule 3 — The Limited Data Set (LDS) Alternative

A Limited Data Set removes only 16 of the most identifying fields but retains dates and geographic units smaller than state. An LDS requires a signed Data Use Agreement (DUA) and is only permissible for research, public health, or care operations. It is NOT Safe Harbor — it trades less protection for greater data utility.

🔎 What Safe Harbor Does and Does NOT Guarantee

Safe Harbor compliance is binary: either all 18 identifier categories have been addressed, or they haven't. It does not measure residual re-identification risk from the remaining QIs {F, 1952, 980**}. An attacker who knows the population of women born in 1952 living in the 980 ZIP prefix may still narrow down Margaret's identity. Expert Determination (Station 03) is the method that quantifies that residual risk with actual numbers.

💡 Safe Harbor = compliance. Expert Determination = proof. The two pathways serve different needs: Safe Harbor for low-friction data sharing, Expert Determination for releasing datasets where utility must be preserved more precisely.

Re-Identification Risk — Prosecutor, Journalist & Marketer Models

De-identification removes identifiers, but does not prove the dataset is safe. Re-identification risk quantification proves safety — by computing how likely an adversary could link a released record back to a specific individual. Three standardized attacker models each represent a different threat profile, and a dataset must be assessed against all three.

Sweeney's 1997 Finding — Why This Matters

Harvard researcher Latanya Sweeney demonstrated that 87% of the U.S. population could be uniquely identified using only three fields: 5-digit ZIP + full date of birth + sex, cross-referenced against public voter registration rolls. The HIPAA QI field set (ZIP, DOB, sex, race, etc.) was directly motivated by this finding. The lesson: seemingly innocuous quasi-identifiers combine explosively as the number of fields increases.

The Three Attacker Models

Prosecutor Model

The attacker already knows a specific individual is in the dataset and tries to find their record. Models targeted attacks: litigation discovery, insurance fraud investigation, surveillance.

Risk = 1 / min(k)

Even one k=1 record makes prosecutor risk = 1.0 (certainty). This model is the most conservative.

Journalist Model

The attacker draws a random record from the dataset and attempts to identify whoever it belongs to. Models investigative reporting, spot-check re-identification, or opportunistic attacks.

Risk = (#k=1 records) / N

Fraction of the dataset that is perfectly and trivially re-identifiable by chance selection.

Marketer Model

The attacker maximizes total successful re-identifications across the entire dataset. Models commercial data brokers running bulk linkage attacks against purchased auxiliary datasets.

Risk = (1/N) × Σ(1/kᵢ)

Expected fraction of all N records successfully linked — the average success rate.

HIPAA Expert Determination Threshold

The phrase "very small" risk in HIPAA's Expert Determination standard is interpreted in practice as ≤ 0.09 (9%) under the prosecutor model. This implies min(k) ≥ 12, because 1/12 ≈ 0.083 < 0.09. Many organizations target k ≥ 5 for research datasets, with k ≥ 12 reserved for highly sensitive data (HIV, SUD, mental health).

k-Anonymity — Equivalence Classes

k-Anonymity provides a formal guarantee: every record in the dataset is indistinguishable from at least k−1 other records on all quasi-identifier fields. A group of k records sharing the same QI values is called an equivalence class. An attacker who successfully links someone to their equivalence class cannot identify which of the k records is theirs — they face 1-in-k uncertainty.

k-Anonymity Formal DefinitionDataset D satisfies k-anonymity iff for every record rᵢ:
|Q(rᵢ)| ≥ k   where   Q(rᵢ) = { rⱼ ∈ D : QI(rᵢ) = QI(rⱼ) }
Q(rᵢ) is the equivalence class of rᵢ — all records in D sharing the same QI values.
Two Tools to Achieve k-Anonymity

Generalization — Replace precise QI values with broader categories (DOB → decade, ZIP → 3-digit prefix). Merges records into shared equivalence classes. First choice: preserves data utility.

Suppression — Remove an entire record when generalization alone cannot place it into a group of size k. Last resort: costs one full data point but may be necessary for demographic outliers (very old, very young, rare combinations).

⚙ k-Anonymity Decision Rules
  • For each record, find all other records in D that share the same values on every QI field.
  • If ≥ k−1 other records match → the record satisfies k-anonymity. Retain it.
  • If 0 other records match (k=1 — record is alone in its cell) → try more aggressive generalization first. If still alone → suppress the record.
  • Minimum k is specified in the Data Use Agreement. Common values: k=2 (research), k=5 (public release), k=12 (sensitive diagnoses, matching the 0.09 prosecutor threshold).

l-Diversity — Protecting Sensitive Attributes Within Groups

k-Anonymity ensures an attacker cannot single out a specific individual — but it says nothing about what the attacker can infer about that individual's sensitive attributes. l-Diversity plugs this gap: within each equivalence class, there must be at least l distinct values of every sensitive attribute. This prevents an attacker from knowing the diagnosis even when they know the equivalence class.

l-Diversity DefinitionAn equivalence class Q satisfies l-diversity if:
|{ distinct sensitive values in Q }| ≥ l
A dataset is l-diverse if EVERY equivalence class satisfies this condition.
The Homogeneity Attack — Why k-Anonymity Alone Fails

Suppose all 5 patients in an equivalence class have been diagnosed with Bipolar Disorder. An attacker who links any person to that class immediately knows their diagnosis with 100% certainty — even though they cannot identify which of the 5 patients it is. k-Anonymity provided identity protection; l-diversity is needed for attribute protection. Without l-diversity, sensitive diagnoses like HIV, opioid use disorder, or mental illness can still be inferred directly from group membership.

📌 Scenario

A privacy officer is reviewing the k=2 dataset released from Station 04. She asks: "Even though no individual can be singled out, can an attacker who links a person to Group β infer what diagnosis that person has?" This question requires computing the l-diversity of each equivalence class.

⚙ How to Read l-Diversity
  • l = 1: All patients in the group share the same sensitive value. Attacker can infer the attribute with 100% certainty. Complete attribute failure.
  • l = 2: At least 2 distinct sensitive values. Attacker has at most 50% certainty per guess. Minimum acceptable in most frameworks.
  • l = 3+: Three or more distinct values. Maximum attacker certainty = 1/l. Stronger protection, but harder to achieve in small groups.
  • The required minimum l is typically specified in the same Data Use Agreement that sets minimum k.

Conditional Probability & Quasi-Identifier Re-ID Risk

k-Anonymity and Safe Harbor focus on the structure of the dataset. Conditional probability analysis asks a different question: given the quasi-identifiers visible in a specific record, how large is the population of real people who could plausibly be that record's subject? This anonymity set size directly determines re-identification probability.

QI Re-Identification ProbabilityP(re-id) ≈ 1 / |anonymity set|
where |anonymity set| = # people in the population sharing all the same QI values as this record
Note: this assumes uniform distribution within the set — a conservative approximation.
QI Intersection Collapse — How Anonymity Sets Shrink

Each quasi-identifier field an attacker observes multiplies their re-identification probability. Adding a 4th QI field that has 200 possible values (e.g., a specific clinic) shrinks the anonymity set by a factor of up to 200. This is why Safe Harbor removes so many fields — the intersection effect is exponential, not additive.

📌 Scenario

⚙ How to Compute Anonymity Set Size
  • Start with the full population of the geographic area covered by the QI.
  • Apply each QI field as a filter: multiply by the fraction of the population matching that field's value.
  • The remaining count is the anonymity set. Re-id probability = 1 / anonymity set size.
  • Each additional QI field the attacker knows acts as a further divisor on the anonymity set.

Unicity — How Unique Are Records in the Wild?

Unicity measures the average number of individuals per quasi-identifier combination cell across the full dataset. It answers: "In this released dataset, do most QI combinations belong to exactly one person, or to many?" When each QI cell contains only one person, the dataset has high unicity — every record is effectively a fingerprint.

Unicity Formulau = N / (Sᵢ × Sⱼ × ... × Sᵏ)
where N = total records, Sᵢ = # distinct values per QI field
u >> 1 → low unicity (safe). u << 1 → high unicity (dangerous). Critical threshold: u ≈ 1.
The Critical Breakpoint at u = 1

When average per cell falls below 1.0, more QI combination cells are empty than occupied. This means the occupied cells are predominantly singletons — unique individuals. An attacker with auxiliary QI data can link records with near-certainty in a high-unicity dataset, even without any direct identifier.

📌 Scenario

A hospital privacy officer is evaluating whether their 500-patient discharge dataset is safe to publish in its current form. The dataset contains three quasi-identifier fields: Sex, Age Decade, and 5-digit ZIP code. Before applying generalization, she wants to understand the theoretical uniqueness of records under this QI combination. How dense is each combination cell?

⚙ Reading Unicity Results
  • u >> 1 (say, u = 50+): On average, each QI cell contains many patients. Records are well-mixed. Low unicity = good privacy.
  • u ≈ 1: The critical boundary. Half the occupied cells contain only one person. Serious privacy risk.
  • u << 1 (say, u = 0.1): Most occupied cells contain exactly one person. Nearly every record is a singleton fingerprint. Extremely high re-identification risk even without names.
  • Applying generalization (e.g., ZIP-5 → ZIP-3) increases u by reducing Sᵏ, spreading records across fewer, larger cells.

Bayes' Theorem — Updating Re-ID Certainty from Evidence

Bayes' Theorem formalizes how to update a probability estimate when new evidence arrives. In healthcare privacy, it models how a re-identification attacker updates their certainty about a person's identity as they observe matching quasi-identifier fields. In clinical informatics, it explains why a positive test result is often less alarming than patients assume.

Bayes' TheoremP(H|E) = [P(E|H) × P(H)] / P(E)
where P(E) = P(E|H)×P(H) + P(E|¬H)×P(¬H) [Law of Total Probability]

P(H) = prior probability of hypothesis before seeing evidence
P(E|H) = likelihood of evidence given hypothesis is true
P(E|¬H) = probability of evidence when hypothesis is FALSE (false positive rate)
P(H|E) = posterior probability — the updated belief after seeing evidence
📌 Scenario

An ED physician receives a positive result on a rapid sepsis biomarker test for a patient presenting with fever. The test has known performance characteristics from published studies. Before ordering invasive treatment, the physician applies Bayes' Theorem to determine the actual probability that this patient truly has sepsis, given the positive test result. This same mathematical structure applies to re-identification attacks: the "hypothesis" becomes "this record belongs to Person X" and the "evidence" is matching QI field values.

⚙ Variable Notation Reference
  • P(H) = prior = baseline probability before any test/evidence. Here: sepsis prevalence in ED patients with fever.
  • P(E|H) = likelihood = sensitivity of the test = P(positive test | truly has sepsis).
  • P(E|¬H) = false positive rate = P(positive test | does NOT have sepsis). Equal to 1 − specificity.
  • P(H|E) = posterior = the probability we actually care about: P(sepsis | positive test).
  • Denominator P(E) = total probability of getting a positive test, from all causes. Computed via the Law of Total Probability: P(E|H)×P(H) + P(E|¬H)×P(¬H).

🔎 Why the Answer Is Surprising and Why It Matters
  • Without the test, the physician estimates 20% chance of sepsis (the prior).
  • With the positive test result, the posterior is 69.2% — a substantial update, but not certainty.
  • A ~31% chance remains that the positive test is a false alarm. The physician should still correlate with clinical signs.
  • Re-identification parallel: An attacker starts with P(H) = 1/N (prior = one in N records). Each matching QI field acts as evidence E, updating the attacker's certainty via Bayes. After k matching fields, the posterior may approach 1.0 (near-certain identification). This is why each additional QI field multiplies re-id risk so aggressively.
💡 The prior matters enormously. Low-prevalence conditions require much higher test specificity to be clinically useful.

Fellegi-Sunter Probabilistic Record Linkage

When two datasets lack a shared unique identifier (no MRN, no SSN), records must be linked probabilistically. Fellegi-Sunter linkage computes a log-odds score for each candidate record pair based on how many fields agree and how diagnostically useful each field's agreement is. The final score determines whether the pair is a match, non-match, or requires manual review.

Fellegi-Sunter Weight Formula (per field)wᵢ = log₂(mᵢ / uᵢ)
Total score W = Σ wᵢ (sum over all agreeing fields)
mᵢ = P(field i agrees | records ARE a true match)
uᵢ = P(field i agrees | records are NOT a match — random coincidence rate)
Weight Interpretation

Positive weight = agreement on this field is evidence for a match. Larger weight = stronger evidence. A large weight results from high mᵢ (true matches almost always agree on this field) and/or low uᵢ (random coincidence agreement is very rare). Typical thresholds: W < 0 → non-match; 0 ≤ W < 8 → possible match (manual review); W ≥ 8 → auto-link. Exact thresholds are calibrated per dataset and use-case.

📌 Scenario

Riverside Medical Center and a local specialty pharmacy are collaborating on an opioid outcomes study. Both organizations have records for David Okafor (P2) but use completely different patient ID systems. Before they can merge the datasets for analysis, they must determine whether the record in each system belongs to the same person — without exchanging raw patient names or SSNs. Fellegi-Sunter probabilistic linkage is the clinical informatics standard for this problem.

⚙ How to Read the Weight Table
  • mᵢ (match rate): How often do true record pairs agree on this field? DOB is very high because people rarely enter their DOB incorrectly.
  • uᵢ (unmatch rate): How often do random non-matching records agree on this field by coincidence? Last name is lower u than ZIP code because names are more unique.
  • wᵢ = log₂(m/u): Larger positive value = more informative field. A field with m=0.95 and u=0.01 contributes log₂(95)≈6.6 bits of evidence.
  • Total score W = sum of weights for all agreeing fields. Compare to the thresholds to decide: link, review, or reject.

Bloom Filter PPRL — Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage

Traditional record linkage requires sharing raw PII (names, DOBs, SSNs) between organizations. Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage (PPRL) using Bloom filters solves this: each organization converts its PII into a compact bit array using deterministic hash functions. The bit arrays are compared without ever sharing the underlying PII. Two records are declared a match if their bit arrays are sufficiently similar.

Bloom Filter PPRL — Key FormulasBigrams(text) = set of 2-character overlapping substrings, e.g. "DAVID" → {_D, DA, AV, VI, ID, D_}
For each bigram: set bit positions h₁(bigram) and h₂(bigram) in a B-bit array

Dice similarity = 2 × |bits set in BOTH arrays| / (|bits in array A| + |bits in array B|)
Dice ≥ 0.75 → probable match; Dice < 0.5 → non-match; 0.5–0.75 → human review
Why Bloom Filters Work for Privacy

The hash functions are deterministic (same input always gives same bit positions), so two organizations hashing the same name will produce the same bit pattern. But the mapping is not reversible — you cannot recover the original name from the bit array. The privacy guarantee holds as long as the bit array is large enough (≥1,000 bits in production) and the hash salt is kept secret.

📌 Scenario

Riverside Medical Center and the state cancer registry both have records for Priya Subramanian (P5) but use incompatible patient ID systems and cannot share raw names under their data sharing agreement. Using Bloom filter PPRL, both organizations hash Priya's first name independently into 16-bit test arrays. They then compare the bit arrays using Dice similarity to determine whether their records belong to the same patient — without either party ever seeing the other's raw data.

⚙ Reading the Dice Similarity Score
  • Dice = 1.0: Identical bit patterns — almost certainly the same name from the same person.
  • Dice ≥ 0.75: High overlap — probable match. May be same name with minor spelling variation.
  • Dice 0.5–0.75: Moderate overlap — requires human review before linking.
  • Dice < 0.5: Low overlap — almost certainly different names. Non-match.
  • Production note: Real PPRL uses 1,000–2,000 bit arrays with k=3–5 hash functions per bigram to reduce both false positives and sensitivity to short names. The 16-bit example here is for illustration only.

Differential Privacy — Mathematically Guaranteed Privacy

Differential Privacy (DP) provides the strongest formal privacy guarantee in data science: an algorithm's output is mathematically indistinguishable whether or not any single individual's data was included. This is achieved by adding precisely calibrated random noise before releasing any aggregate statistic. DP eliminates membership inference attacks — an attacker cannot determine whether a specific person's data contributed to the result, even with unlimited background knowledge.

Laplace Mechanism for Count QueriesM(D) = f(D) + Laplace(Δf / ε)
f(D) = the true query answer (e.g., raw patient count)
Δf = sensitivity = maximum change in f when one person is added/removed
ε (epsilon) = privacy budget; smaller ε = stronger privacy, more noise
For COUNT queries: Δf = 1 always (one person changes count by ±1 maximum)
ε (Epsilon) — The Privacy Budget Explained

Epsilon controls the tradeoff between privacy and utility. ε = 0.1 provides very strong privacy but introduces substantial noise (a 1,000-patient count might be reported as 963 or 1,041). ε = 1.0 is the standard research threshold — moderate protection, moderate noise. ε = 10 provides weak protection; the noise is small but an attacker with auxiliary data may still infer membership. Many organizations treat ε as a finite budget that gets depleted with each published query on the same dataset.

📌 Scenario

Riverside Medical Center is preparing its annual public health report. One statistic to publish is: "How many patients in our system have a recorded mental health diagnosis?" The privacy officer insists this count be protected by differential privacy. Why? Even a simple count can confirm or deny whether a specific named individual is in the dataset — a membership inference attack. If an attacker knows the count would be 342 without their target, and the published count is 343, they can infer their target is included. DP prevents this inference by adding calibrated noise.

⚙ Reading DP Results — What the Noise Means
  • The Laplace mechanism draws a random noise value from a Laplace distribution with scale = Δf/ε = 1/ε.
  • The published value = true count + noise. The noise is sometimes positive (inflates the count), sometimes negative (deflates it).
  • The expected error magnitude ≈ 1/ε. For ε=0.5: expected error ±2. For ε=1: expected error ±1. For ε=10: expected error ±0.1.
  • An attacker who sees the published count cannot determine the true count precisely, and therefore cannot confirm or deny membership of any individual.
  • Δf = 1 for all count queries because adding or removing any single person from a dataset changes the count by exactly 1 — no more, no less.

🔎 Choosing the Right Epsilon
  • ε = 0.1–0.5: Strong privacy. Suitable for very sensitive queries (mental health counts, HIV prevalence). Significant noise may distort small counts.
  • ε = 1.0: Standard research threshold. Widely used in academic DP literature. Balanced tradeoff.
  • ε = 5–10: Weak privacy. Often used when the dataset is already low-risk and utility is paramount. Not suitable for sensitive attributes.
  • Privacy composition: Each additional DP query consumes from the total epsilon budget. Publishing 10 queries each with ε=1.0 consumes a total budget of ε=10.0. Budget exhaustion must be tracked and enforced per dataset release.
💡 Differential privacy is the only method in this tutorial that provides protection against an attacker with unlimited auxiliary knowledge. k-Anonymity, l-diversity, and Safe Harbor all assume a bounded attacker. DP does not — it works regardless of what other data the attacker possesses.
