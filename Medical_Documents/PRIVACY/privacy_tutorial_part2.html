Conditional Probability & Quasi-Identifier Re-ID Risk

In healthcare privacy, an attacker asks: "Given these observable data fields, which specific patient does this record belong to?" Observable fields — ZIP code, age, sex — are quasi-identifiers (QIs). They don't individually identify a person, but their intersection can collapse the candidate set to a handful or even one individual.

The Re-Identification Probability

An attacker computes P(record = person X | quasi-identifiers). Using Bayes' Theorem, this equals 1 divided by the size of the population sharing those same QI values. The smaller the anonymity set, the higher the re-identification probability.

QI Re-ID ProbabilityP(re-id) = (# records in dataset matching QI) / (# people in population with same QI)
Simplified: P(re-id) ≈ 1 / |anonymity set|

P5 Priya Subramanian has QI = {Female, born 1972, ZIP 98055 → prefix 980}. How many women born in 1972 live in ZIP area 980? That is her anonymity set.

BEFORE — Known QI Values for P5
Population in 3-digit ZIP area 980: ~420,000 residents
Female: ~52% → 420,000 × 0.52 = 218,400
Born in 1972 (age ~52): roughly 1 of 70 birth-year cohorts → 218,400 / 70 ≈ 3,120
Anonymity set size ≈ 3,120 women born in 1972 in this ZIP area
OPERATION — P(re-id) Calculation
P(record is Priya | QI = {F, 1972, 980**}) = 1 / 3,120 ≈ 0.000321 (≈0.032%)
AFTER — Risk Assessment
Re-id probability: 1/3,120 ≈ 0.032% → VERY LOW with 3-digit ZIP + birth year
BUT: if attacker adds a 4th field (e.g., "diagnosed with Bipolar Disorder, age 51-52"),
and that diagnosis is rare (1 in 200 women aged 52):
Set shrinks: 3,120 / 200 = 15.6 → P(re-id) ≈ 1/16 = 6.25%
One additional QI multiplies risk by 200×.
This illustrates why Safe Harbor removes so many fields. Each additional quasi-identifier an attacker possesses multiplies their re-id probability, often dramatically. Diagnosis codes themselves can be highly specific auxiliary information.

Unicity & Re‑Identification Risk

Unicity answers a simple but alarming question: "What fraction of people in a dataset can be uniquely identified by just a few data fields?" The answer is often: almost everyone.

If you have enough categorical attributes, the number of possible combinations grows multiplicatively. Once the number of possible combinations exceeds the number of people, most people end up alone in their cell — perfectly re-identifiable.

Unicity — Key RelationshipsTotal possible QI cells = (values of field 1) × (values of field 2) × … × (values of field k)
Average people per cell = N / (total cells)
If avg ≪ 1 → most cells have 0 or 1 person → high unicity → high risk
BEFORE — Dataset Description
Dataset: 500 patient records
QIs: Sex (2 values) × Age Decade (7 values) × Diagnosis Category (20 ICD chapters)
Total cells = 2 × 7 × 20 = 280
Average occupancy = 500 / 280 ≈ 1.79 patients per cell
ANALYSIS — Unicity With 3 Fields
With avg 1.79 per cell, ~37% of cells have exactly 1 person (Poisson model).
These ~140 patients are uniquely identifiable — unicity is MODERATE to HIGH.
AFTER — Adding 1 More QI Field Multiplies Risk
QI Fields UsedTotal CellsAvg per CellUnicity Level
Sex + Age + Dx (3 fields)2801.79Moderate
Sex + Age + Dx + ZIP-3 prefix (4 fields)1,4000.36HIGH ❌
Each additional QI field is a multiplier on the number of cells. The N stays constant while cells multiply. Adding a seemingly harmless field — a timestamp, a clinic ID, a visit count — can dramatically increase re-identification risk.

Bayes' Theorem — Applied to Re-Identification

Bayes' Theorem is the mathematical engine behind re-identification probability. When an attacker has a hypothesis ("this record belongs to Priya Subramanian") and observes evidence (QI values that match), Bayes' Theorem tells them how much to update their confidence.

Bayes' TheoremP(H | E) = [ P(E | H) × P(H) ] / P(E)
where P(E) = P(E|H)×P(H) + P(E|¬H)×P(¬H)
The Four Terms — In Re-ID Context

P(H) — Prior: fraction of the population that could plausibly be the target. If 3,000 women aged 50-59 share a QI profile, P(H) = 1/3,000.
P(E|H) — Likelihood: probability the auxiliary evidence matches, given the hypothesis is correct.
P(E|¬H) — False positive rate: how often auxiliary evidence matches a random non-target.
P(H|E) — Posterior: the updated probability after seeing the evidence. What the attacker is computing.

A clinical context to verify the theorem intuitively before applying it to re-identification.

SCENARIO
ED sees 500 patients/day. 5% have sepsis. Rapid test: sensitivity 90%, FP rate 8%.
A patient tests positive. What is the probability they truly have sepsis?
STEPS
P(H) = 0.05  |  P(E|H) = 0.90  |  P(E|¬H) = 0.08
P(E) = 0.90×0.05 + 0.08×0.95 = 0.045 + 0.076 = 0.121
P(H|E) = 0.045 / 0.121 ≈ 0.372 (37.2%)
RESULT
Even with a positive test, only ~37% chance of sepsis.
The low prior (5%) pulls the posterior down. Same dynamic governs re-id:
when the target is 1 person in 3,000, even strong auxiliary evidence
may yield a modest posterior probability.

Fellegi–Sunter Probabilistic Record Linkage

Two healthcare systems — a hospital and a pharmacy — each have David Okafor's records but no shared patient ID. To combine them for research, we need to determine whether two records belong to the same patient. This is record linkage.

The Fellegi–Sunter (1969) model compares records field-by-field and computes a weight measuring how likely two records are to be a true match vs. a coincidence. Weights are in "bits" of evidence — analogous to log-likelihood ratios.

Fellegi–Sunter Weight for Field iIf AGREE: wᵢ = log₂( mᵢ / uᵢ )
If DISAGREE: wᵢ = log₂( (1 − mᵢ) / (1 − uᵢ) )
Total weight W = Σ wᵢ across all compared fields
m-probability and u-probability

mᵢ = P(field agrees | records are a TRUE match). High m means true matches usually agree on this field (e.g., DOB matches 95% of the time, despite occasional data-entry errors).
uᵢ = P(field agrees | records are NOT a match). Low u means agreement is very unlikely by chance alone (e.g., full DOB randomly agrees ~0.003% of the time across 50 years of dates).

BEFORE — Two Records Being Compared
FieldHospital RecordPharmacy RecordAgreement

COMPUTATION — m=0.92, u=0.005 (name); m=0.95, u=0.01 (DOB)
w_name = log₂(0.92/0.005) = log₂(184) ≈ 7.52 (strong positive evidence)
w_dob = log₂(0.95/0.01) = log₂(95) ≈ 6.57 (strong positive evidence)
Total W = 7.52 + 6.57 = 14.09

Name contributed slightly more weight than DOB because u_name (0.5%) is lower than u_dob (1%)
→ name agreement is more "surprising" by chance, so it carries more information.
In a real PPRL scenario, these weights would be computed on Bloom filter encodings of the name and DOB — never on the raw text. See Station 10 for how Bloom filters enable this without exposing the actual values.

Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage — Bloom Filters

Station 09 showed Fellegi-Sunter weights require comparing field values. But sharing raw names and DOBs across organizations creates obvious privacy risk. Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage (PPRL) with Bloom filter encoding allows matching without ever exchanging plaintext identifiers.

Bloom Filter Encoding — 4 Steps

① Take a patient name (e.g., "CHEN") and break it into overlapping character bigrams: CH, HE, EN.
② Hash each bigram to one or more positions in a fixed-length bit array (e.g., 20 bits).
③ Set those bit positions to 1. All others stay 0. This bit array is the Bloom filter.
④ Two organizations compare their Bloom filters using a similarity measure — without sharing raw names.

Dice Coefficient — Measuring Bloom Filter SimilarityDice(BF₁, BF₂) = 2 × |BF₁ ∩ BF₂| / (|BF₁| + |BF₂|)
Where |BF| = count of 1-bits in the filter, |BF₁ ∩ BF₂| = positions that are 1 in BOTH
BEFORE — Raw Name
Name: "CHEN" (uppercase)
Bigrams: CH, HE, EN
OPERATION — Hash to 20-bit filter (simplified, 1 hash per bigram)
Hash(CH) → position 3 → set bit 3 to 1
Hash(HE) → position 7 → set bit 7 to 1
Hash(EN) → position 12 → set bit 12 to 1

BF(CHEN) = 00100010000010000000 (bits 3,7,12 set)
|BF(CHEN)| = 3 (three 1-bits)
AFTER — Bloom Filter Comparison
Compare BF(CHEN) with BF(CHAN):
CHAN bigrams: CH, HA, AN → positions 3, 9, 15 → |BF(CHAN)| = 3
|BF(CHEN) ∩ BF(CHAN)| = 1 (only bit 3 shared: the CH bigram)
Dice = 2×1 / (3+3) = 2/6 ≈ 0.333

Compare BF(CHEN) with BF(CHEN) [exact match]:
|intersection| = 3, Dice = 2×3 / (3+3) = 1.0 → perfect match
Raw names "CHEN" and "CHAN" are never shared — only the Bloom filters. In practice, larger bit arrays (1,000-2,000 bits) and multiple hash functions (k=3-5) are used, making it computationally infeasible to reverse-engineer the original name from the filter.

Differential Privacy — Formal Disclosure Avoidance

Differential Privacy (DP) is the gold standard for aggregate query protection. It provides a mathematical guarantee: adding or removing any one individual's record changes the output distribution by at most a factor of eε, where ε is the privacy budget. An attacker who sees the output cannot determine whether any specific individual was in the dataset.

Laplace Mechanism — The Most Common DP ToolTo answer a query f on dataset D with privacy guarantee ε:
Release: f(D) + Laplace(b)   where b = Δf / ε
Δf = sensitivity (max change in f from adding/removing one person)
E[|noise|] = b     Std dev of noise = √2 × b
Sensitivity and the Privacy-Utility Tradeoff

Sensitivity Δf — For a counting query ("how many patients have diabetes?"), Δf = 1, since one person can change the count by at most 1.

Privacy budget ε (epsilon) — Smaller ε = stronger privacy guarantee but more noise added. ε = 0.1 is very strong (very noisy). ε = 10 is weak (minimal noise). Values of ε = 0.5 to 2.0 are common in research.

The tradeoff: more privacy (smaller ε) always means less utility (more noise). There is no free lunch in differential privacy.

Query: "How many Meridian patients have a mental health diagnosis?" True answer: 2 (Priya=Bipolar, David=OUD). We add Laplace noise to protect individual membership.

BEFORE — Query and True Answer
Query: COUNT(patients WHERE dx IN mental health codes)
True answer: 2 patients (P5 Priya: Bipolar F31.9; P2 David: OUD F11.20)
Sensitivity Δf = 1 (adding/removing one patient changes count by 1)
Privacy budget: ε = 0.5 (moderately strong protection)
OPERATION — Laplace Mechanism
b = Δf / ε = 1 / 0.5 = 2.0
E[|noise|] = b = 2.0
Std dev of noise = √2 × 2.0 ≈ 2.83

Released answer = 2 + Laplace(2.0)
Example outcome: could release "3.4" or "0.2" or "-0.8" (rounded or clipped to non-negative)
AFTER — Differential Privacy Guarantee
The released noisy count protects individual membership.
An attacker cannot determine whether Priya (or David) specifically is in the dataset.
The true count 2 is obscured by noise with std dev ≈2.83 — a large relative perturbation for such a small number.
With larger counts (e.g., 5,000 diabetic patients), the same noise (std dev 2.83) is negligible.
DP is powerful for aggregate statistics but cannot protect individual records directly. It is typically applied at the output layer — when releasing summaries, counts, or model parameters — not during the data storage phase. The Meridian Cohort (only 6 patients) shows the noise clearly; real datasets with thousands of patients absorb it much better.

The Defense-in-Depth Principle

No single technique in this tutorial is sufficient on its own. Safe Harbor removes identifiers but doesn't measure risk. k-Anonymity prevents singling out but not inference. l-Diversity prevents inference but not skewness attacks. Differential privacy protects aggregates but not individual records. Real-world healthcare data protection requires layering multiple techniques, aligned to the specific data type, release mechanism, and anticipated attacker model.
