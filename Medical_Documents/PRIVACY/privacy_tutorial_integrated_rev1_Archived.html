De-Identification — General Concepts

De-identification is the process of removing or transforming information in a dataset so individuals cannot be readily identified. It enables secondary use of healthcare data — research, analytics, public health — without exposing patient privacy.

De-identification is not anonymization. Anonymization implies identity can never be recovered. De-identification reduces re-identification risk to an acceptable level. This distinction matters legally and ethically under HIPAA.

Three Categories of Data Fields

Identifier Classification

Direct Identifiers — Alone or nearly alone they identify an individual: names, SSNs, MRNs, phone, email, full addresses, biometrics. These are suppressed (removed entirely).

Quasi-Identifiers (QI) — Individually innocuous but identifying in combination: DOB, ZIP, sex, age, race/ethnicity. These are generalized (made less precise) to preserve analytical utility while reducing linkability.

Sensitive Attributes — The information we want to analyze: diagnoses, labs, medications. Retained but only after QIs are sufficiently anonymized.

Three De-Identification Techniques

TechniqueWhat It Does & When to Apply
SuppressionCompletely removes a field. Applied to direct identifiers and records that cannot meet privacy standards through generalization alone.
GeneralizationReplaces precise values with broader categories. DOB → year; 5-digit ZIP → 3-digit prefix; exact age → decade. Preserves utility while reducing linkability.
PerturbationAdds controlled random noise to numeric values. Used in differential privacy (Station 11). Useful for aggregate statistics where exact counts must be obscured.

HIPAA Safe Harbor — The 18 Identifier Rule

HIPAA provides two de-identification pathways. Safe Harbor lists exactly 18 categories of identifiers that must be removed. No statistical expertise required — remove all 18 and the dataset is compliant. The second pathway, Expert Determination, allows a statistician to certify "very small" risk, but requires formal analysis (Station 03).

Three Critical Safe Harbor Rules

Rule 1 — ZIP Code Population Threshold

3-digit ZIP prefixes may be retained only if the area contains ≥ 20,000 people (U.S. Census). If fewer than 20,000, the prefix must become 000.

Rule 2 — Date Generalization & Age 90+ Rule

All patient-associated dates must be reduced to year only. Patients aged 90 or older must also have their birth year removed and replaced with Age 90+.

Rule 3 — The Limited Data Set (LDS) Alternative

A Limited Data Set removes only 16 of the most identifying fields but retains dates and geographic units smaller than state. Requires a signed Data Use Agreement (DUA). NOT Safe Harbor — less protection, greater utility.

Re-Identification Risk — Prosecutor, Journalist & Marketer Models

De-identification removes identifiers but does not prove the dataset is safe. Re-identification risk quantification proves it — by modeling what different adversaries could accomplish. Three attacker models are standard in the field, and a dataset can appear low-risk under one model while failing another.

Sweeney's 87% Finding

In 1997, Latanya Sweeney (Harvard) demonstrated that 87% of the U.S. population could be uniquely identified using only 5-digit ZIP + full date of birth + sex, cross-referenced against public voter registration rolls. The lesson: quasi-identifiers combine explosively.

The Three Attacker Models

Prosecutor Model

Attacker already knows a specific individual is in the dataset. Worst case — targeted attack or litigation.

Risk = 1 / min(k)

Even one k=1 record makes prosecutor risk = 1.0 (100%).

Journalist Model

Attacker samples randomly and tries to re-identify whoever they land on. Models investigative reporting or spot-checks.

Risk = (#k=1 records) / N

Fraction of the dataset that is perfectly re-identifiable.

Marketer Model

Attacker maximizes total re-identifications across the dataset. Models commercial data brokers or bulk linkage attacks.

Risk = (1/N) × Σ(1/kᵢ)

Average success rate across all N records.

HIPAA Expert Determination Threshold

"Very small" risk is interpreted in practice as ≤ 0.09 (9%) under the prosecutor model, meaning min(k) ≥ 12.

k-Anonymity — Equivalence Classes

k-Anonymity ensures every record is indistinguishable from at least k−1 other records on all quasi-identifier fields. A group of k records sharing the same QI values is an equivalence class. An attacker who links someone to a class faces 1-in-k uncertainty.

k-Anonymity Formal DefinitionDataset D satisfies k-anonymity iff for every record rᵢ, its equivalence class Q(rᵢ) contains ≥ k records.
Q(rᵢ) = { rⱼ ∈ D : QI(rᵢ) = QI(rⱼ) }
Two Tools to Achieve k-Anonymity

Generalization — Replace precise QI values with broader categories. Cost: reduced data precision.

Suppression — Remove records that cannot be merged into a group of size k. Last resort; costs an entire data point.

l-Diversity — Protecting Sensitive Attributes Within Groups

k-Anonymity ensures an attacker cannot single out a specific individual — but says nothing about what they can infer about that individual's sensitive attributes. l-Diversity plugs this gap: within each equivalence class, there must be at least l distinct values of every sensitive attribute.

l-Diversity DefinitionAn equivalence class Q satisfies l-diversity if:
|{ distinct sensitive values in Q }| ≥ l
A dataset is l-diverse if every equivalence class satisfies this condition.
The Homogeneity Attack — Why k-Anonymity Alone Fails

If all patients in a class share the same sensitive diagnosis — say, all five have Bipolar Disorder — an attacker linking a person to that group immediately knows their diagnosis with 100% certainty, even without singling them out. l-Diversity prevents this by demanding sensitive values be spread across each group.

Conditional Probability & Quasi-Identifier Re-ID Risk

An attacker asks: "Given these observable data fields, which specific patient does this record belong to?" Observable fields — ZIP, age, sex — are quasi-identifiers. Their intersection collapses the candidate set to a handful or even one individual.

QI Re-ID ProbabilityP(re-id) ≈ 1 / |anonymity set|
where the anonymity set = # people in the population sharing those same QI values
QI Intersection Collapse

Each additional quasi-identifier multiplies the precision of an attacker's knowledge. Adding a diagnosis code, clinic ID, or visit timestamp can shrink an anonymity set from thousands to single digits.

SCENARIO — P5 Priya Subramanian: QI = {Female, born 1972, ZIP 98055 → prefix 980}
Population in ZIP area 980: ~420,000 residents
Female: ~52% → 420,000 × 0.52 = 218,400
Born in 1972 (age ~52): roughly 1/70 birth-year cohorts → 218,400/70 ≈ 3,120
Anonymity set ≈ 3,120 women born in 1972 in this ZIP area
P(re-id) = 1 / 3,120 ≈ 0.032%
Adding 1 More QI Field: If attacker knows patient has Bipolar Disorder (1 in 200 women aged ~52):
Set shrinks: 3,120 / 200 = 15.6 → P(re-id) ≈ 6.25%
One additional QI multiplied risk by ~200x.
This illustrates why Safe Harbor removes so many fields. Each additional quasi-identifier an attacker possesses multiplies their re-id probability. Diagnosis codes are themselves highly specific auxiliary information.

Unicity & Re‑Identification Risk

Unicity answers: "What fraction of people in a dataset can be uniquely identified by just a few data fields?" When the number of possible QI combinations exceeds the number of people, most people end up alone in their cell — perfectly re-identifiable.

Unicity — Key RelationshipsTotal QI cells = (values of field 1) × (values of field 2) × … × (values of field k)
Avg people per cell = N / total cells
If avg ≪ 1 → most cells have 0 or 1 person → high unicity → high re-id risk

Bayes' Theorem — Applied to Re-Identification

Bayes' Theorem is the mathematical engine behind re-identification probability. When an attacker has a hypothesis ("this record belongs to Priya") and observes evidence (QI values that match), Bayes tells them how much to update their confidence.

Bayes' TheoremP(H | E) = [ P(E | H) × P(H) ] / P(E)
P(E) = P(E|H)×P(H) + P(E|¬H)×P(¬H)
The Four Terms in Re-ID Context

P(H) — Prior: fraction of the population that could be the target. If 3,000 women aged 50-59 share a QI profile, P(H) = 1/3,000.
P(E|H) — Likelihood: probability the auxiliary evidence matches if the hypothesis is correct.
P(E|¬H) — False positive rate: how often evidence matches a random non-target.
P(H|E) — Posterior: the updated probability after seeing the evidence.

ED: 500 patients/day. 5% have sepsis. Rapid test: sensitivity 90%, FP rate 8%.
A patient tests positive. What is the probability they truly have sepsis?

P(H)=0.05, P(E|H)=0.90, P(E|¬H)=0.08
P(E) = 0.90×0.05 + 0.08×0.95 = 0.045 + 0.076 = 0.121
P(H|E) = 0.045 / 0.121 ≈ 0.372 (37.2%)

Even with a positive test, only ~37% chance of sepsis. The low prior (5%) pulls the posterior down. Same dynamic governs re-id: when the target is 1 in 3,000, even strong auxiliary evidence yields a modest posterior.

Fellegi–Sunter Probabilistic Record Linkage

Two healthcare systems each have David Okafor's records but no shared patient ID. To combine them for research, we need to determine whether two records belong to the same patient — this is record linkage. The Fellegi–Sunter (1969) model compares records field-by-field and computes a weight measuring evidence of a true match vs. coincidence.

Fellegi–Sunter Weight for Field iIf AGREE: wᵢ = log₂( mᵢ / uᵢ )
If DISAGREE: wᵢ = log₂( (1 − mᵢ) / (1 − uᵢ) )
Total weight W = Σ wᵢ across all compared fields
m-probability and u-probability

mᵢ = P(field agrees | records are a TRUE match). High m means true matches usually agree on this field.
uᵢ = P(field agrees | records are NOT a match). Low u means agreement is very unlikely by chance alone.

COMPUTATION — m_name=0.92, u_name=0.005; m_dob=0.95, u_dob=0.01

Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage — Bloom Filters

Station 09 showed Fellegi-Sunter weights require comparing field values. But sharing raw names and DOBs across organizations creates obvious privacy risk. Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage (PPRL) with Bloom filter encoding allows matching without ever exchanging plaintext identifiers.

Bloom Filter Encoding — 4 Steps

① Take a patient name (e.g., "CHEN") and break it into overlapping character bigrams: CH, HE, EN.
② Hash each bigram to one or more positions in a fixed-length bit array (e.g., 20 bits).
③ Set those bit positions to 1. All others stay 0. This bit array is the Bloom filter.
④ Two organizations compare Bloom filters using a similarity measure — without sharing raw names.

Dice Coefficient — Measuring Bloom Filter SimilarityDice(BF₁, BF₂) = 2 × |BF₁ ∩ BF₂| / (|BF₁| + |BF₂|)
|BF| = count of 1-bits; |BF₁ ∩ BF₂| = positions that are 1 in BOTH
Name: "CHEN" → Bigrams: CH, HE, EN
Hash(CH) → bit 3; Hash(HE) → bit 7; Hash(EN) → bit 12
BF(CHEN) = 00100010000010000000 → |BF(CHEN)| = 3

Compare BF(CHEN) vs BF(CHAN): BF(CHAN) has bits 3, 9, 15 → |BF(CHAN)|=3
|intersection| = 1 (only bit 3: the CH bigram)
Dice = 2×1/(3+3) = 2/6 ≈ 0.333

Compare BF(CHEN) vs BF(CHEN) [exact]: intersection=3 → Dice = 2×3/(3+3) = 1.0 ✓
Raw names "CHEN" and "CHAN" are never shared — only the Bloom filters. In practice, 1,000-2,000 bit arrays with multiple hash functions make reverse-engineering computationally infeasible.

Differential Privacy — Formal Disclosure Avoidance

Differential Privacy (DP) is the gold standard for aggregate query protection. It provides a mathematical guarantee: adding or removing any one individual's record changes the output distribution by at most a factor of eε, where ε is the privacy budget. An attacker who sees the output cannot determine whether any specific individual was in the dataset.

Laplace Mechanism — The Most Common DP ToolRelease: f(D) + Laplace(b)   where b = Δf / ε
Δf = sensitivity (max change in f from adding/removing one person)
E[|noise|] = b   Std dev of noise = √2 × b
Sensitivity and the Privacy-Utility Tradeoff

Sensitivity Δf — For a counting query, Δf = 1, since one person can change the count by at most 1.
Privacy budget ε (epsilon) — Smaller ε = stronger privacy but more noise. ε=0.1 is very strong (very noisy). ε=10 is weak (minimal noise).
The tradeoff: more privacy always means less utility. There is no free lunch in differential privacy.

Query: COUNT(patients WHERE dx IN mental health codes)
True answer: 2 patients (P5 Priya: Bipolar F31.9; P2 David: OUD F11.20)
Sensitivity Δf=1 (counting query). Privacy budget: ε=0.5.

b = Δf / ε = 1/0.5 = 2.0
E[|noise|] = b = 2.0   Std dev of noise = √2 × 2.0 ≈ 2.83
Released answer = 2 + Laplace(2.0) → could be "3.4" or "0.2" or "-0.8"
DP is powerful for aggregate statistics but cannot protect individual records directly. Applied at the output layer when releasing summaries, counts, or model parameters. For N=6 patients, the noise is large relative to the answer — real datasets with thousands of patients absorb it much better.

The Defense-in-Depth Principle

No single technique in this tutorial is sufficient on its own. Safe Harbor removes identifiers but does not measure risk. k-Anonymity prevents singling out but not inference. l-Diversity prevents inference but not skewness attacks. Real-world healthcare data protection requires layering multiple techniques, aligned to the specific data type, release mechanism, and anticipated attacker model.
